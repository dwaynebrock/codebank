The Art of Blacksmithing

Blacksmithing: hammer and anvil

Blacksmithing is one of the world's oldest arts. Desire to learn blacksmithing is nearly as old as the discovery of harnessing fire.

Written descriptions of blacksmithing, or more properly, metal forging, penetrate our sciences, art, older religious ideas, in sum our sense of being human. Blacksmithing is a truly human art.

We have many mythological heroes as blacksmiths (1,2). The biblical Tubal-Cain, the ancient Egyptian neter Ptah, the ancient Syrian Kothar-wa-Kthasis, Odin and the Dwarves of Norse myth, Weiland the Smith of the English, the Hindu Tvastar, the Greek Hephaestus, the Roman Vulcan, the Slavic Svarog, the Celtic Goibhniu, the Nigerian Ogun, and Amakuni of medieval Japan are names that myth and legends continue to hand down from one generation to another.

Nor is any region of the globe excluded; any human culture that knew how to harness fire discovered blacksmithing. It is a stage of development we all knew at one time or another in our evolution.

Something very tactile and solid brings us to blacksmithing; the feel of the heat and the hammer in one's hands, the shapes of the metal used as material before and after formation, the sweat we feel from ourselves from the heat and smoke. The unmistakable feeling of pride in a metal item once made. It echoes through our past and when done it affects our present.

Though at one point blacksmithing almost died out due to the advancement of metal machining techniques in the modern industrial age, a rather potent reforming of blacksmithing has happened in the 21st century through the popularity of blacksmithing in the arts and as a social activity. We do owe a debt of thanks to the many artisans and proponents of culture that kept blacksmithing alive until it sprang back.

A search of youtube will bring up any number of blacksmithing instructional videos and artistic work examples. Alec Steele presents well produced videos that are a bit over the top, but entertaining. Chandler Dickinson, Black Bear Forge, and Christ Centered Ironworks present good sets of foundational knowledge lessons for aspiring blacksmiths. Some unusual variations exist, such as shown on the Youtube channel Glen GS Tongs. This creator does all blacksmithing on the outside ledge of an apartment, and does so in a sitting manner to adapt to health issues. The innovative spirit of this blacksmith's approach is quite amazing. Some fascinating innovations exist out there and make for interesting content.

Knifemaking, which is a related art to blacksmithing has also caught popularity and is well-known through such television shows as Forged In Fire, and a flurry of content on Youtube. A revival of blacksmithing is definitely in motion.
